The media layer

The gap between
what science shows
and what you read.

This is not about fake news or deliberate misinformation. It is a structural problem in how health and science journalism works — and it produces systematic, measurable distortions in what the public believes.

The novelty bias
New overrides consistent
A single new study challenging established consensus gets coverage. The 47 confirming it don't. Journalism rewards novelty. Science rewards replication. The incentives run in opposite directions.
The dramatic finding bias
Effect size gets lost
"Doubles the risk" sounds alarming. But if baseline risk is 0.02%, doubling it is 0.04%. Relative risk without absolute risk is almost always misleading — and relative risk is what gets printed.
The industry funding problem
Who paid for the study
Industry-funded nutrition research is four times more likely to produce findings favourable to the funder. This rarely appears in the headline. It changes how much weight the finding deserves.
The design hierarchy problem
Not all studies are equal
A meta-analysis of 40 RCTs and a single observational study of 200 people are both reported as "a study found." They are not remotely equivalent. Verity weights them accordingly.

For any topic, Verity retrieves every relevant paper from the last five years across three independent academic databases — Semantic Scholar, PubMed, and OpenAlex. It then separately retrieves five years of news coverage on the same topic from major outlets.

The gap between those two distributions — where science actually lands versus where media places the topic — is the divergence signal. For many well-covered health topics, it is substantial and consistent. Media coverage of nutrition topics leans measurably more optimistic than the quality-weighted scientific evidence.

That gap is not random. It has a direction, a magnitude, and it shapes what millions of people believe.

An example
Search "red meat and cancer." The quality-weighted scientific consensus scores at approximately 95% concern — strong, consistent association with colorectal cancer across multiple large meta-analyses. Media coverage of the same topic over five years scores around 60–65% concern. The gap exists because alarming findings get more coverage than the steady accumulation of confirmatory evidence.
The social media layer

A misquoted finding
travels at the speed
of a trend.

The distortions introduced by science journalism are real. But they are compounded by what happens when those already-simplified stories enter social media — where they are compressed, decontextualised, and amplified by engagement mechanics that have nothing to do with accuracy.

What the study said
A prospective cohort study of 1,200 adults found that those reporting higher ultraprocessed food intake had a hazard ratio of 1.14 (95% CI 0.98–1.32) for all-cause mortality after adjusting for 14 confounders. The association was attenuated and non-significant after full adjustment.
What went viral
"Scientists confirm ultraprocessed food literally kills you." 2.3M views. 140K shares. The 95% confidence interval straddling 1.0 and the non-significant result after adjustment were not included in any of the 47 quote-tweets that collectively reached 8M people.

This is not an anomaly. It is the default behaviour of information on platforms optimised for engagement. Alarming, simple, and emotionally resonant beats nuanced, probabilistic, and conditional — every single time. The algorithm does not penalise inaccuracy. It rewards attention.

The result is a population whose beliefs about health, nutrition, medicine, and risk are shaped less by the actual scientific record and more by which findings happened to generate viral engagement in the week they were published. A single misrepresented study can embed a belief so firmly that years of contradicting literature cannot move it.

The scientific community produces its most important work over decades. Social media operates on a cycle of days. The mismatch is not a bug — it is the entire structure of the problem.

The partial quote
A study shows X in population Y under condition Z. Social media removes Y and Z. The finding becomes universal when it was conditional.
The preliminary finding
Pre-print, n=80, no peer review, one lab. Goes viral. The three subsequent failed replications do not.
The influencer pipeline
A study gets a quote in a health newsletter. A fitness influencer reposts it without the study. The audience has no idea it started as research.
The retraction gap
A retracted study keeps circulating years after retraction. The original post: millions of impressions. The retraction notice: 340 views.
On certainty

Knowing the evidence
is pointing somewhere
is not the same as
knowing why.

One of the most important distinctions in science communication — and one that is almost never made — is the difference between a consistent signal and a proven cause.

Take coffee. The evidence that regular coffee consumption is associated with lower all-cause mortality is, at this point, about as consistent as observational nutrition research gets. Dozens of large cohort studies, across multiple populations, with millions of subjects, all pointing the same direction.

But almost all of that evidence is observational. We cannot randomly assign people to drink or not drink coffee for twenty years and measure who dies. So the association is robust, the direction is clear — but the causal mechanism is not fully established.

This is not the same as not knowing. It is knowing something with a specific kind of confidence — strong enough to influence a rational decision, not strong enough to make a causal claim. Verity distinguishes these two things explicitly, because collapsing them into a single "we're not sure" is one of the most misleading things you can do with scientific evidence.

Directional confidence
How consistently does the evidence point one way? Strong directional confidence means many independent studies agree. This is a meaningful signal regardless of whether causation is established.
Causal certainty
Can we establish mechanism and causation, not just association? For many long-term health questions, this is structurally impossible to achieve — not because the signal is weak, but because the required study design would be unethical or impractical.
Genuine disagreement
High-quality studies pointing in opposite directions. This is a real scientific debate — not a limitation of evidence, but evidence of a genuinely contested question. Verity flags this separately.

The question "should I drink coffee?" and the question "does coffee definitively cause longevity benefits?" are not the same question. Science can answer the first even while working on the second.

Over 40 million people consult ChatGPT for health information every day. Billions more run health searches on Google. Both tools are genuinely useful — and both have structural limitations that make them unreliable when the question is about the actual state of scientific evidence. These aren't bugs. They're how the tools were designed.

Search engine
Google
Returns pages ranked by authority and clicks — not by scientific accuracy or study quality.
Popularity ≠ accuracy. The top result is the most authoritative-seeming webpage, not the most reliable representation of the evidence.
AI Overviews make it worse. A Guardian investigation found Google's AI summaries gave dangerous advice on cancer, psychosis, and eating disorders — and the same query produced different answers each time.
No study hierarchy. Google cannot distinguish a meta-analysis of 40,000 participants from a blog post summarising a press release. Both can rank first.
SEO shapes results. Sites optimised for search rank above sites optimised for accuracy. The incentives run in opposite directions.

The bigger picture

Holding your beliefs
loosely.

There is something deeply counterintuitive about scientific literacy: the more you understand how science works, the less certain you become about any individual finding — and the more confident you become in the overall direction of a body of evidence.

A layperson reads a headline and forms a belief. A scientist reads the same headline and immediately wants to know the sample size, the design, who funded it, whether it has been replicated, what the effect size was, whether the confidence interval crossed null, and whether it fits with the mechanistic plausibility from related fields.

These are not the same epistemic operations. The layperson ends up more certain. The scientist ends up more calibrated. Calibrated is better. Not because uncertainty feels good, but because decisions made on calibrated uncertainty tend to be better than decisions made on false confidence.

The goal is not to know less. It is to know with appropriate confidence — and to update that confidence honestly as the evidence accumulates.

This is what Verity is trying to make possible for anyone, not just scientists. The ability to look at a question and see the whole picture: how consistent is the evidence, what kind of studies support it, where does media coverage diverge, and what would it take to change the assessment.

Not a verdict. A view of the landscape. The kind that actually helps you make better decisions — and helps you hold the next headline a little more loosely.
