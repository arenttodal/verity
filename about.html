What Verity does

Verity answers a question nobody else is properly answering: what does the accumulated peer-reviewed evidence actually show, and how far has media coverage drifted from it? The consensus score comes from a deterministic algorithm, not an AI opinion. The media divergence layer is something no existing tool provides.

The problem

The gap between what research shows
and what reaches people.

Over 4 million scientific papers are published every year. A fraction gets covered. A fraction of that coverage is accurate. And a fraction of that accuracy survives long enough to shift what people actually believe. The problem isn't that the research doesn't exist.

Access
Most high-quality research sits behind paywalls, written for specialists. The evidence exists in abundance. It just has no practical path to the people who need it.
Translation
Journalism rewards novelty and drama. A surprising new finding gets coverage; the 40 studies quietly confirming established consensus don't. The bias has a consistent direction and it compounds over years.
Synthesis
Nothing currently shows you both where the quality-weighted scientific evidence lands and how far media coverage has moved from it. That's the specific gap Verity fills.

The divergence between scientific consensus and media narrative has a direction, a magnitude, and it shapes what millions of people believe. Verity makes it visible.

What it returns

The whole picture,
not the loudest study.

Type any health or science question in plain language. In 20 to 30 seconds, Verity returns six things that no existing tool combines.

01
Quality-weighted consensus score
A spectrum score from Concern to Benefit, computed by a deterministic formula, not an AI opinion. Each paper is weighted by study design, bias, precision, relevance and independence.
02
Two kinds of confidence
Direction and certainty are always shown separately. A topic can have a strong consistent signal while resting on observational evidence only. Those are different things and Verity treats them as different things.
03
Plain-language synthesis
Written by Claude after the scoring is done. Every number in the summary comes from the computed output. Claude cannot invent a finding that wasn't in the papers.
04
Evidence strip
Every study rendered as a mark on the spectrum. Meta-analyses get wider capsule shapes; RCTs and cohort studies get thinner lines. Height reflects quality weight. The shape of the distribution often tells you more than the headline score.
05
Media divergence layer
Five years of coverage from major outlets on the same topic, scored and placed on the same spectrum as the science. The gap between where research lands and where journalism sits becomes something you can see.
06
Source sidebar
Click any mark on the evidence strip. See the abstract, quality score, framing analysis and related articles. Every number links to a real paper. The scores are computed before Claude touches the data.
The Scoring Formula
w = Design Prior × Bias Weight × Precision Weight × Relevance Weight × Independence Weight
c = Direction × Magnitude × w
score = 100 × Σ(c) / Σ|w × Magnitude| → bounded [-100, +100]

Where it sits
Examine.com has spent 15 years proving that people will pay for trusted, ad-free evidence synthesis. Their subscribers pay $19 to $29 a month for curated summaries without a media divergence layer, without a live scoring engine, and without a visual that shows you where headlines drift from research. Evidence intelligence is the category: not academic search, not general AI chat, but a tool built specifically to show you what the accumulated science says and where public understanding has diverged from it.
Why now

Why this moment
matters.

01
The search habit is in flux
General AI search has disrupted where people go for information, and habits haven't settled yet. Vertical tools that are more trustworthy and more specific than a general chatbot have an unusual opening right now. That opening exists because people are actively looking for something better, not because they've already found it.
02
Trust in health journalism is genuinely lower
It took a significant and probably permanent hit over the last five years. At the same time, genuine interest in understanding what research actually shows has grown. Those two things together create exactly the audience Verity is built for.
03
The infrastructure cost dropped to near zero
PubMed, Semantic Scholar and OpenAlex all opened high-quality free APIs between 2020 and 2023. Two years ago, building this would have required expensive data partnerships. The economics are now completely different.

The category is there to be claimed. That's not something you can say indefinitely.
